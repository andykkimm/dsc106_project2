Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Andrew Kim, Justin Lee, Victor Chen — akk006@ucsd.edu, jal174@ucsd.edu, vic006@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Black individuals are disproportionately the subjects of police misconduct allegations compared to their representation in the general population.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

    Decision 1 – Data Cleaning and Selection (Score: +2, Earnest)
    We filtered the dataset to include only relevant columns such as complainant_ethnicity, allegation, and board_disposition, and removed missing or low-frequency entries. This ensures the visualization accurately reflects racial distribution without distortion. This choice is fully earnest since it prioritizes data integrity and transparency. Decision 2 – Using NYC 2020 Census as a Baseline (Score: +1.5, Earnest)
    We compared allegation proportions with population proportions from the 2020 NYC Census to contextualize disparities. This helps viewers understand over- and underrepresentation relative to the city’s demographics. While this introduces an external dataset (which might have minor temporal mismatch), it’s still largely earnest because it strengthens interpretability and fairness.
  • Visualization Choice: A side-by-side bar chart was selected to clearly display Allegations (%) vs. Population (%) by race, making over- and underrepresentation immediately visible and emphasizing evidence of racial disparity.
    • Decision 3 – Side-by-Side Bar Chart Comparison (Score: +1.5, Earnest)
    We used grouped bars to compare Allegations (%) and Population (%) by race. This layout makes it easy to visually identify which groups are disproportionately represented. The clear visual contrast supports the proposition effectively while maintaining honesty — no scale manipulation or exaggeration is used. Alternative options (like stacked bars) were less direct and harder to interpret.
